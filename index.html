Hi, I'm Florence. A childhood fascination with p-zombies — whether there's really someone home behind a pair of eyes — sent me looking for the answer in the wiring: through neurobiology and computational neuroscience, and into building the devices that let us listen to the brain and talk back. Since then my work has spanned neural interfaces, cybernetics design, and cognitive augmentation — from molecular neurobiology and nanoscale materials to open-source neurotech, wireless cortical implants, clinical brain–computer interfaces, and imaging, on into industry.

I make all sorts of doodads. From memory enhancing wearables, brain-to-brain communication implants, and biomimetic prosthetic limbs, to sundial sculptures, smart furniture, and artificial senses — I love bringing insights from one field into another.

I've worked up and down the scale — from single calcium channels and stretchable materials, to sub-millimeter wireless implants, clinical brain–computer interfaces, and the imaging and prosthetics that put them to use. My research lives in multiscale neural recording and edge ML as compression for wireless BCI — and I care as much about understanding a thing as making it work.

An avid 3D-printing fan, and a rabid TTRPG one. Let’s chat!

Networked microscale implants · Brown

Neurograins

// NURMIKKO LAB · BROWN UNIVERSITY · 2015–2021

Before one big implant, we tried a swarm of tiny ones. Neurograins are hundreds of independent, sub-millimeter “grains” scattered across the cortex — each recording or stimulating on its own, kept in sync by a wearable skin-patch radio. I built recording and stimulation ASICs, the wireless networking that coordinates a thousand nodes at once, and the neural decoding, across benchtop and in-vivo studies. This was my Brown era, all at once — the BrainGate clinical program, computational neuroscience at the Carney Institute, and robotics at the Humanity-Centered Robotics Initiative — where alongside the grains I prototyped “neural dust” for brain-to-brain links, modeled personalized neurostimulation to quiet epileptic cortex, and built multi-extruder 3D printers, smart furniture, and a force-feedback exoskeleton.
